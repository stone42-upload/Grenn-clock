The Doomsday Clock:
89 Seconds to Midnight

By The Green Clock Editorial Team • July 2026 • 5 min read

The Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists has kept the Doomsday Clock at 90 seconds to midnight, and recently adjusted it to 89 seconds, marking the closest the clock has ever been to global catastrophe. But what exactly does this mean, and how does it relate to our daily consumption habits and the push for a circular economy?

A Brief History of the Clock

Created in 1947 by scientists who had worked on the Manhattan Project, the Doomsday Clock is an internationally recognized design that conveys how close we are to destroying our civilization with dangerous technologies of our own making. Initially focused solely on the threat of nuclear war, the Bulletin began factoring climate change into its calculations in 2007.

"The Clock has become a universally recognized indicator of the world's vulnerability to catastrophe from nuclear weapons, climate change, and disruptive technologies in other domains."

Why 89 Seconds?

The decision to move the clock to 89 seconds to midnight—the closest it has ever been—was driven largely by the ongoing war in Ukraine, the modernization of nuclear arsenals globally, and the failure of global leaders to adequately address the compounding effects of the climate crisis.

Specifically regarding climate, the Science and Security Board noted that global greenhouse gas emissions continue to rise, and current pledges to reduce them are insufficient to prevent the worst impacts of global warming. The extreme weather events of the past few years are no longer anomalies; they are the new baseline.

The Linear Economy is a Threat Multiplier

While nuclear threats often grab the headlines, the slow-moving catastrophe of climate change and ecological collapse is deeply intertwined with our economic models. The current "take-make-dispose" linear economy is directly responsible for over 45% of global greenhouse gas emissions (the rest coming from energy production).

  • Resource Extraction: Drives deforestation, habitat loss, and massive energy consumption.
  • Overproduction: Creates artificial demand, leading to factories running 24/7 to produce items destined for landfills.
  • Waste Management: Methane emissions from landfills and toxic runoff from e-waste directly threaten global health.

Turning Back the Clock: The Circular Solution

If the Doomsday Clock is a metaphor for urgency, the Circular Economy is the blueprint for a solution. By rethinking how we design, produce, and consume goods, we can drastically reduce our planetary impact.

Transitioning to renewable energy is only half the battle. To turn back the clock, we must embrace a system where materials are kept in use, products are designed for longevity and repair, and natural systems are regenerated.

What can be done today?

The Bulletin emphasizes that the Clock is not a prediction of the future, but a warning to prompt action. Every fraction of a degree of warming avoided matters. Every ton of material kept out of a landfill matters. By demanding circular practices from corporations and adopting them in our own lives, we can push the hands of the clock back away from midnight.
